# PureTask — California Gig-Economy Attorney Consultation Agenda

**Document 18**
**Version:** 1.0
**Meeting Duration:** 2 hours
**Meeting Type:** Initial structural review of independent-contractor classification under California law

This document is designed to **maximize the value** of a 2-hour consultation with a California gig-economy / employment attorney. A 2-hour meeting is short. Wasted on background context that should have been read in advance, it produces vague advice. Used well, it produces actionable risk assessments and structural recommendations.

The plan:

1. **Send pre-read materials 5-7 days before the meeting** so the attorney bills less time on basics.
2. **Run a structured agenda** with timed sections and prepared questions.
3. **Capture decisions immediately** in the post-meeting template, not from memory three days later.

### C. What to Bring to the Meeting

- Printed copy of Doc 06 (Pro IC Agreement) with sticky notes on specific concerns
- This agenda document
- A blank notebook + pen (or laptop in note-taking mode)

### D. Mindset for the Meeting

- **Push for specific advice, not general framing.** Attorneys default to "it depends." Push back: "Given my specific facts, what would you do?"
- **Decisions over discussion.** End each section with a decision or a clear next step.
- **Be honest about messy facts.** If you've sent marketing copy describing "our team" of cleaners, tell the attorney. Hiding facts gets you bad advice.
- **Don't try to negotiate fees during this meeting.** Focus on the content.

## Part 2 — The Meeting Agenda (2 Hours)

### Section 1 — Background and Current Status of CA Gig-Economy Law (15 min)

**Goal:** Make sure the attorney's read of *recent* California caselaw aligns with what you've planned for. Catch any law changes since you drafted Doc 06.

**Questions:**

1. What are the most material developments in California gig-economy law in the last 12 months that affect a cleaning marketplace specifically?
2. How are courts currently applying the ABC test (Lab. Code § 2775) to marketplaces that match consumers with individual service providers?
3. Have there been any class actions or settlements involving cleaning-service marketplaces (Handy, Homejoy, others) that I should learn from?
4. How does Cal-PAGA (Lab. Code § 2698) currently interact with arbitration agreements after *Adolph v. Uber*?
5. Has anyone successfully defended the Prong B "outside the usual course of business" prong for a cleaning marketplace? If so, what facts were decisive?

**Watch for:** If the attorney's answers reveal recent unfavorable rulings or settlements you weren't aware of, this could change your overall structure decision (see Section 4).

---

### Section 2 — Pro IC Agreement Review (Doc 06) (25 min)

**Goal:** Get specific feedback on what's strong, what's weak, and what to change in the Pro IC Agreement.

**Questions:**

1. Walk me through your top 3 concerns with the Pro IC Agreement (Doc 06) under the ABC test.
2. Is the Section 3.3 ABC Test Acknowledgment language enforceable, or is it overreach that a court will ignore?
3. The agreement reframes PureTask's "usual course of business" as marketplace technology, payment-processing facilitation, dispute mediation, and trust-and-safety (Section 3.3 Prong B). Is this a defensible characterization, or will a court look at substance over form?
4. Are the "Pro's Rights and Freedoms" in Section 4 (schedule, rates, decline-without-penalty, work-for-others, substitute) sufficient to satisfy Prong A?
5. Is Section 12 (Indemnification, specifically misclassification indemnity) enforceable against an individual Pro? Or will a court strike it as unconscionable?
6. Is the 14-day cure period in Section 15.3 too long, too short, or appropriate?
7. The Section 16 "no restrictive covenants except the Platform Attribution Fee" (which applies to Clients, not Pros) — is this fully compliant with § 16600 / SB 699, or do I need to remove it entirely?
8. Anything I should ADD that's missing?
9. Anything I should REMOVE that creates more risk than it mitigates?

**Watch for:** The attorney should be able to articulate **3 specific edits** they would make. If they only say "looks fine," push harder or get a second opinion.

---

### Section 3 — Operational Practices Review (15 min)

**Goal:** Beyond paper, what operational behaviors strengthen or undermine IC defense?

**Questions:**

1. What marketing language, copy, or business practices most commonly undermine IC defense? Things to avoid?
2. The Reliability Indicator (Doc 06 Section 4.4 and Doc 01 Section 12 — neutral signals like on-time arrival, completion rate, dispute history) — is this safe to show on Pro profiles, or does it create control evidence?
3. The voluntary trust-and-safety features (GPS check-in, before/after photos, in-app time tracking) — under what circumstances do these become evidence of control over the manner and means of work?
4. The flat per-Booking Platform Fee (vs. a percentage commission) — does this strengthen or weaken IC defense compared to a typical marketplace?
5. The 30-day Platform Attribution Fee for off-Platform circumvention — is this enforceable, or does it look like restraint of trade despite the liquidated-damages framing?
6. Cleaning Pros entering Client homes raises trust-and-safety questions. What operational practices (background checks, identity verification, mandatory reporting) can I require WITHOUT crossing into control over the manner of work?
7. Where does PureTask's customer support sit on the line between "marketplace neutrality" and "employer-like supervision"?

**Watch for:** Specific operational changes the attorney recommends. Capture these for implementation, not just in your head.

---

### Section 4 — Strategic Alternatives and Multi-State (20 min)

**Goal:** Decide whether to launch with the current IC structure in California, or to consider alternatives.

**Questions:**

1. **Given my current Pro IC Agreement and operational structure, what's your professional estimate of the probability that:**
   - A plaintiff's law firm will file a class action within the first 3 years of public launch?
   - That class action would survive a motion to dismiss?
   - PureTask would survive a Prong B challenge at summary judgment?
   - The case would settle vs. proceed to verdict?

2. **What's the realistic worst-case financial exposure** if PureTask loses on Prong B in California? (Back-wages, PAGA penalties, attorneys' fees, settlement order of magnitude.)

3. **Would you recommend** launching with the current IC structure in California, or alternatives:
   - **Option A**: Status quo. Launch with IC structure, mitigate operationally, accept Prong B risk.
   - **Option B**: W-2 employment model in California, IC in TX and FL. Higher cost, lower legal risk in CA.
   - **Option C**: Delay California launch entirely. Launch in TX/FL first, return to CA when Pro density justifies a fee floor that economically supports W-2.
   - **Option D**: Some other hybrid you have not considered.

4. If we choose Option A, what's the **fastest, cheapest way to fail well** if a class action arrives — i.e., quick settlement, asset protection, reorganization?

5. What's your view of **multi-state expansion**: launching in TX and FL gives me revenue diversification away from CA risk. How aggressively should I prioritize that?

6. Is **federal preemption** under the Federal Arbitration Act still a viable defense path for individual Pro claims in 2026? Or has the CA Supreme Court's view in *Adolph* effectively neutered it?

**Watch for:** A specific recommendation. If the attorney refuses to recommend, ask: "Given everything you know, would you personally launch this in California today?"

### Section 5 — Other Compliance Topics (15 min)

**Questions:**

2. **Background checks (Doc 07 FCRA Disclosure + Doc 07A/07B Pre/Adverse Action)**: Does the standalone FCRA disclosure meet the *Gilberg* standard? Are the California Fair Chance Act / AB 2188 / SB 700 protections correctly addressed?

7. **Anything I am NOT asking about that I should be?**

## Part 3 — Watch-Outs During the Meeting

These are the moments to **slow down, take notes, and push for specifics**:

### When the Attorney Says "It Depends"
Push back: "What facts make it depend? Given my facts, which way does it lean?"

### When the Attorney Says "We Could Argue..."
Push back: "Argued by whom, in what setting, with what likelihood of success?"

### When the Attorney Says "Most Courts..."
Push back: "Most California courts? Recent California courts? Cite the case."

### When the Attorney Says "I've Seen This Before"
Capture the specific situation, the outcome, and what was learned.

### When the Attorney Avoids a Direct Recommendation
Ask: "I understand you can't guarantee outcomes, but as my counsel, what would you do in my position?"

### When You Don't Understand a Term
Ask immediately. Don't pretend. "Can you explain what *Borello* means here, in plain English?"

## Part 6 — If You Walk Away with Only One Thing...

The single most important question to get a clear answer to is:

> **"Given the Pro IC Agreement in Doc 06 and the operational practices I have described, what is your honest professional estimate of the probability that PureTask survives an ABC test challenge on Prong B in California?"**

If the attorney says **>70%**: Launch with current structure, with the recommended operational changes. Accept the residual risk.

If the attorney says **40-70%**: Make all recommended changes, then re-assess. Consider delaying CA launch by 6 months while you build TX/FL revenue.

If the attorney says **<40%**: Seriously consider Option B (W-2 in CA) or Option C (skip CA initially). The legal risk likely outweighs the CA revenue opportunity at pre-launch stage.

## Part 8 — Finding the Right Attorney

If you have not yet selected an attorney, look for:

1. **California-licensed employment / gig-economy attorney** with **plaintiff-side experience** (someone who has sued companies like yours; they know the playbook better than purely defense-side attorneys).
2. **Experience with marketplace / platform clients** (Uber-Lyft-Instacart-DoorDash counsel, even if not those exact companies).
3. **Familiarity with AB 5, *Dynamex*, *Adolph v. Uber***.
4. **No conflicts** with Stripe, Checkr, or your existing/potential investors.
5. **Reasonable rates** for your stage (~$500-1,500/hour).

**Where to find:**
- Referrals from other marketplace founders
- California Lawyers Association employment law section directory
- Avvo and Martindale-Hubbell ratings (for vetting)
- LinkedIn searches for "AB 5 attorney" or "California gig economy"

**Red flags:**
- Refuses to share recent caselaw familiarity
- No marketplace clients in their portfolio
- Says they can "guarantee" or "guarantee win" on ABC test (no one can)
- No engagement letter, no clear fee structure
- Promises to "handle everything" without disclosure of scope
