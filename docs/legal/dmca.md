# PureTask --- Cease-and-Desist and DMCA Templates

**Document 13** **Version:** 1.0

This document contains **four enforcement templates** PureTask LLC may use to protect its intellectual property and contractual rights:

1.  **Template A --- Cease-and-Desist (Trademark Infringement)** --- for unauthorized use of the "PureTask" word mark, "Dash" mascot, "P" house mark, or related trade dress.
2.  **Template B --- Cease-and-Desist (Platform Attribution Fee / Off-Platform Circumvention)** --- for Clients who book a Pro off-Platform within the 30-day attribution window.
3.  **Template C --- DMCA Takedown Notice** --- sent to third-party platforms (App Stores, social media, hosting providers) when infringing content appears there.
4.  **Template D --- Response to DMCA Counter-Notice** --- for when an alleged infringer pushes back on a takedown.

**These templates are operational and not part of the customer-facing Platform documents. Use under guidance of legal counsel for any matter of consequence.**

# Template A --- Cease-and-Desist (Trademark Infringement)

> **HOW TO USE THIS TEMPLATE**
>
> Send to a third party who is using the PureTask name, logo, Dash mascot, or related trade dress without authorization. Typical scenarios:

- A competitor uses "PureTask" or a confusingly similar name (e.g., "PureTasks Cleaning," "PureTask Pro Cleaning," etc.);
- A look-alike app uses Dash mascot or similar hummingbird-with-cleaning-products imagery;
- A copycat marketplace mimics PureTask's trade dress (color palette, layout, signature features);
- A bad-actor Pro or Client misrepresents themselves as employed by or partnered with PureTask.

> **Send by email** to the recipient's known contact and, where higher-stakes, **also by certified mail**. Keep all delivery proof for at least 3 years (the federal trademark statute of limitations is governed by laches; longer is safer).
>
> **Before sending**: (a) confirm PureTask trademark rights (common-law use + ideally a USPTO registration or pending application); (b) gather evidence of the infringing use (screenshots with URLs and timestamps); (c) discuss with IP counsel for anything over a generic copycat.

# Template B --- Cease-and-Desist (Platform Attribution Fee)

> **HOW TO USE THIS TEMPLATE**
>
> Send to a Client who books a Pro off-Platform within 30 days of an active Booking, in violation of Section 15.A of the Terms of Service. Provide internal documentation of the off-Platform booking (Pro confession, Client text exchange, payment confirmation, etc.). Keep the tone professional; this is a fee collection notice, not a hostile dispute. Most Clients pay when reminded that the term is non-negotiable.

# Template C --- DMCA Takedown Notice

> **HOW TO USE THIS TEMPLATE**
>
> Send to a third-party platform (App Store, Google Play, social media, hosting provider, search engine) when infringing **copyrighted** PureTask content (e.g., copied app screenshots, copied marketing copy, copied Dash mascot artwork) appears there.
>
> **Note**: DMCA is for *copyright*. For trademark issues (someone using the name "PureTask"), use Template A and the platform's **trademark complaint** form, not DMCA.
>
> Each major platform has its own DMCA submission form. Use those when they exist; this letter template is for less formal hosts or for cases where you want a written record.
>
> **Required elements** under 17 U.S.C. § 512(c)(3): 1. Physical or electronic signature of authorized agent; 2. Identification of the copyrighted work; 3. Identification of the infringing material with sufficient location detail; 4. Contact information of the complaining party; 5. Good-faith belief statement; 6. Statement under penalty of perjury that information is accurate and you are authorized.
>
> **Risk of Sec. 512(f)**: If you misrepresent that material is infringing, you can be held liable for damages, costs, and attorneys' fees. Don't use DMCA as a censorship tool.

# Template D --- Response to DMCA Counter-Notice

> **HOW TO USE THIS TEMPLATE**
>
> When an alleged infringer files a counter-notice (claiming their content does not infringe), the platform must restore the content unless PureTask files suit within 10--14 business days. This template helps decide whether to (a) sue, (b) negotiate, or (c) let the content go.
>
> Most often the right call is to **let the content go** unless the infringement is significant. The downside of filing suit is real cost and discovery. Save lawsuits for clear, willful, commercial infringement.

# Operational Reminders

### Trademark Watch

Consider engaging a **trademark watch service** (e.g., Markify, Corsearch, Thomson CompuMark) after USPTO registration. \~\$200-1,500/yr. Identifies competitors filing similar marks before they register, allowing PureTask to oppose at the USPTO instead of suing post-registration.

### Documentation Habits

Maintain a **brand-protection log** with: - Date and source of each potential infringement spotted; - Screenshots with URLs and timestamps; - Action taken (none, monitoring, C&D, lawsuit, etc.); - Outcome.

### Don't Use C&Ds Frivolously

Trademark and copyright C&Ds are powerful but get treated harshly by courts when used to suppress speech, criticism, or legitimate competition (e.g., a YouTube reviewer using your name to criticize PureTask is **nominative fair use**, not infringement). Streisand-effect risk is real. Send C&Ds only when: - The infringing use is clear and commercial; - The risk to PureTask's brand is real; - You have evidence and a good-faith basis.

### Designated DMCA Agent Registration

Don't forget: **PureTask must register a DMCA Designated Agent with the U.S. Copyright Office** at **dmca.copyright.gov** to receive DMCA protections under § 512. Registration is \$6, renewed every 3 years. Without this registration, PureTask **cannot rely on the § 512 safe harbor** when User Content infringes someone else's copyright.

*This document is a collection of general templates. They are not legal advice. Engage IP counsel for any non-trivial enforcement action. False or overreaching C&Ds and DMCA notices can themselves create liability --- under Section 512(f) for DMCA, and under California Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16 (anti-SLAPP) for state law claims. Use sparingly and substantively.*
